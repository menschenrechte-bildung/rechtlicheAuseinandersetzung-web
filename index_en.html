ECHR Application

State School Coercion?

Only with quality control and child-friendly remedies.

Introduction and Purpose of this Statement

This Additional Statement is submitted pursuant to Rule 47 § 2(a) of the Rules of Court to supplement and elaborate on the facts and legal arguments summarized in the official ECHR Application Form (Sections E and F). It is designed to provide the Court with the necessary context and detailed legal reasoning regarding a systemic constitutional and human rights failure within the educational administration and the judiciary of the Federal Republic of Germany.

The Tripartite Relationship and the Systemic Rights Vacuum

This case does not concern a standard dispute over administrative non-compliance. Rather, it exposes a fundamental, systemic imbalance within the German educational and judicial framework regarding the tripartite relationship between the child (as an independent bearer of human rights), the parents (as the primary holders of the duty of care), and the State (as the supervisor of the educational system).

In a democratic society, this tripartite relationship must remain in a careful equilibrium. The State's authority to enforce compulsory school attendance is not an absolute right of physical custody over children. It is constitutionally and internationally justified solely as a means to achieve a legitimate purpose: to guarantee the child’s right to effective education and integration.

However, the respondent State has systematically dismantled the essential safeguards that keep this relationship in balance:

  1. The Abandonment of Quality Assurance: By completely suspending all regular external evaluations of public schools since 2015 (see Annex 23, p. 117), the executive is operating its school monopoly in a qualitative vacuum. The State demands physical submission to its institutions under threat of imprisonment and ruinous fines, while simultaneously refusing to verify whether these institutions meet basic pedagogical and safety standards.
  2. The Procedural Exclusion of the Child: The domestic legal system treats the child as a mere object of administrative enforcement. The child's own distress, academic collapse, and explicit wishes are declared "legally irrelevant" by the courts, and the minor is systematically denied the right to be heard (see Annex 6, p. 19 and Annex 9, p. 34).
  3. The Illusory Nature of Judicial Remedies: The domestic legal framework offers no realistic court path to challenge school quality or pedagogical failures. Under established German administrative law (the Schutznormtheorie), statutory educational goals (§ 1 SächsSchulG) and quality assurance provisions (§ 3a SächsSchulG) are classified as mere "programmatic principles" (Programmsätze) serving the public interest, rather than granting individual, actionable rights (subjektiv-öffentliche Rechte) to students or parents. Consequently, any administrative lawsuit seeking to enforce these standards would be dismissed as inadmissible for lack of standing. Even if such a path were theoretically open, the duration of administrative litigation (typically 1.5 to 3 years) renders it completely ineffective to address acute child welfare hazards. Furthermore, the minor child, who is the actual bearer of the right to education, lacks independent procedural standing to bring such actions, leaving him entirely dependent on parental representation while being subjected to direct state coercion. Concurrently, in the repressive enforcement proceedings, the domestic courts refuse to consider school defects at all, declaring them legally irrelevant to the absolute obligation of physical presence (see Annex 6, p. 19).
  4. The Lack of Independent Oversight and Child-Friendly Remedies: Unlike in other European jurisdictions that balance compulsory education with low-threshold protection mechanisms—such as an independent school ombudsman to whom children can directly appeal (e.g., in Sweden or Estonia)—the respondent State provides no independent, out-of-court oversight body. The school supervisory authority (LASUB) is part of the same executive branch that operates the schools, creating an inherent conflict of interest. This is empirically proven by the applicant's own attempts to seek help from the school supervisory authority, which refused to act and instead shielded the school, declaring the statutory goals to be non-binding (see Annex 1, p. 1-2). For a minor child, the lack of an independent, accessible complaint mechanism leaves him entirely defenseless against institutional abuse and pedagogical failure within the state school monopoly.

As a direct consequence of this systemic vacuum, the applicant was trapped in an unavoidable conflict of duties (Pflichtenkollision). Because every administrative, judicial, and private avenue for relief was demonstrably blocked—the school supervisory authority refusing assistance, the courts offering no timely or admissible remedy, and private schools being saturated—the applicant faced a binary choice: either enforce the physical attendance obligation by forcing his child back into an environment that actively endangered his well-being, development, and academic prospects, or fulfill his primary parental duty of care under Article 8 of the Convention and national civil law (§ 1666 BGB) by advising his child to study at home and supporting his subsequent decision to remain absent for self-protection, thereby securing his education through home-study. Under these extreme circumstances, advising the child and supporting his decision to avoid the harmful school environment was not an act of civil disobedience, but the absolute ultima ratio—the sole remaining path to protect the child's right to education. The empirical success of this choice is documented by the state's own graduation certificate (see Annex 24, p. 120-122). Furthermore, the child's successful social and civic integration is evidenced by his voluntary enlistment in the German Federal Armed Forces (Bundeswehr) for Voluntary Military Service starting in September 2026, demonstrating a profound commitment to defend the democratic state. Yet, the State continues to prosecute the applicant, threatening him with a coercive fine of up to EUR 25,000 or coercive detention (see Annex 17, p. 95-96 and Annex 22, p. 107-115). This case, therefore, far transcends a minor administrative dispute over a nominal fine; it represents a continuous, existence-threatening cycle of state coercion designed to financially ruin and psychologically exhaust the applicant. He is thus penalized for successfully resolving a conflict of duties that the State itself created and refused to remedy.

Legal Arguments under the Convention

The complaints do not challenge compulsory schooling as such, but rather the lack of minimum safeguards when enforced through State coercion.

The applicant's complaints are not directed against compulsory schooling as such. They concern the minimum Convention safeguards required when compulsory schooling is enforced through State coercion.

A. THE DOGMATIC FRAMEWORK: THE STATE'S POSITIVE OBLIGATIONS UNDER THE CONVENTION

  1. The Principle of Compulsory Education: The applicant does not challenge the State's general authority to establish and enforce compulsory school attendance. However, under the Convention, this authority is not an absolute right of custody over children. The enforcement of compulsory attendance is legally justified solely as a means to achieve a legitimate purpose: to guarantee the child’s right to effective education and integration. If the State decouples coercion from these educational and protective goals, the enforcement loses its human rights legitimacy.
  1. The Core Safeguards (The Four Positive Duties): The applicant submits that, taken together, Articles 2 of Protocol No. 1, 8 and 13 of the Convention require four minimum safeguards whenever a State enforces compulsory schooling through a near-monopoly of public education:

a) The Duty of Quality Assurance: The State must continuously and empirically monitor that public schools meet basic safety, pedagogical, and quality standards.

b) The Duty of Low-Threshold Independent Oversight: The State must provide an independent, out-of-court oversight mechanism (such as an ombudsman) accessible to both parents and, particularly, minor children.

c) The Duty of Effective Judicial Remedies: The State must ensure access to timely and effective court remedies to challenge educational failures and safety hazards.

d) The Duty to Hear the Child: The State must ensure that the child, as an independent bearer of rights, is heard in all administrative and judicial proceedings affecting their education.

B. SPECIFIC VIOLATIONS OF THE CONVENTION

##### a) Applicable Principles

  1. The first sentence of Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 guarantees a right to education that is effective and of sufficient quality (G.L. v. Italy, no. 59751/15, 2020). While the State has a wide margin of appreciation in organizing its educational system, including the enforcement of compulsory school attendance, this enforcement must always serve the child's interest in receiving a meaningful education. The applicant submits that compulsory attendance cannot remain Convention-compliant where the State continues to enforce attendance despite the absence of safeguards capable of ensuring that such attendance serves the child's educational development and well-being.

##### b) Application to the Present Case

  1. The respondent State has violated this right by completely suspending all regular external school evaluations since 2015 (see Annex 23, p. 117). By abandoning all mechanisms capable of verifying whether compulsory attendance still served its educational purpose, the respondent State continued to enforce attendance independently of any objective assessment of educational quality.
  2. The core violation consists of the State forcing the family to choose between the child's well-being and compulsory school attendance. When Lucas experienced pedagogical conflicts and school anxiety, the State offered no quality-assured alternative, and private replacement schools were unavailable due to regional capacity exhaustion (see Annex 14, p. 79-80). The enforcement of the attendance obligation through administrative fines and the threat of detention placed the applicant in a position where compliance with the school presence mandate directly conflicted with his parental duty to protect his child's development. This disconnect is further illustrated by the school's decision to formally promote Lucas to Class 9 despite 96 unexcused absences and failing grades, showing that enforcement is decoupled from actual education and serves merely as physical presence control (Körperpräsenzpflicht). Despite the child subsequently completing his education successfully through home-study (see Annex 24, p. 120-122), the State has continued to pursue enforcement proceedings against the applicant. The state is thus penalizing the father for choosing a home education path that was the exclusive condition for the child to achieve a higher graduation, while prosecuting him for refusing a state path that led to systemic academic downgrading and failure.

##### a) Applicable Principles

  1. Any interference with the right to respect for private and family life under Article 8 § 1 must be "in accordance with the law," pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society" (Article 8 § 2). The concept of lawfulness requires that domestic law must provide safeguards against arbitrary interference by public authorities. Furthermore, the State has a positive obligation to protect children from physical and psychological harm within public institutions.

##### b) Application to the Present Case

  1. In Germany, school coercion is derived from the state's school supervision mandate under Article 7 Paragraph 1 of the Basic Law (GG). This mandate constitutes a positive constitutional duty to actively supervise schools. The systematic abandonment of statutory supervisory mechanisms (see Annex 23, p. 117) raises serious doubts as to whether the interference retained the quality of lawfulness required by Article 8 § 2.
  2. Under domestic law, the State assumes a positive duty to protect child welfare, intervening in domestic family settings under § 1666 BGB (see Annex 14, p. 75). However, the same protective standards are not applied within the public school system, where the domestic courts declare themselves incompetent to review school conditions, and the supervisory authorities do not intervene to address reported abuses (see Annex 14, p. 75-76).
  3. Under these circumstances, the continuation of coercive measures—specifically the threat of a EUR 25,000 fine or detention—cannot be considered "necessary in a democratic society". The socialization and civic integration of the child have been demonstrated by his subsequent voluntary enlistment in the German Federal Armed Forces (Bundeswehr) (see Annex 24). Furthermore, the state's ongoing practice of cumulative administrative attrition (parallel proceedings regarding vocational school monitoring, administrative fines, and coercive fines) against a single father, continuing even after the child successfully graduated on time, exceeds any legitimate public interest in compliance. It constitutes a form of systemic harassment targeting the financial ruin and psychological exhaustion of the applicant, violating the core of the right to family life under Article 8.

##### a) Applicable Principles

  1. Article 10 protects the right to impart and receive information and ideas without interference by public authority. This protection extends to private consultations and advice within the family unit, especially regarding decisions that directly affect the child's development and well-being.

##### b) Application to the Present Case

  1. The applicant was convicted by the Local Court Dresden explicitly for the content of his private communication with his son (see Annex 6, p. 15, 19). The court treated the applicant's honest advice to Lucas—that home-study was the superior option to protect his development under the school's harmful conditions—as proof of criminal intent. Voicing this assessment and giving parental guidance does not constitute an illegal withdrawal, but rather a legitimate exercise of parental care. By threat of sanction, the state effectively demands that parents act as compliant state agents, forcing them to suppress their honest, factual assessments. Penalizing a father for speaking the truth to his child, especially when that truth is empirically verified by the child's subsequent academic success, is a form of forced communication that violates the core of Article 10.

##### a) Applicable Principles

  1. Under Article 6 § 1, everyone has the right to a fair hearing, which includes the right to a reasoned decision where courts must address the essential arguments of the parties. Furthermore, the procedural safeguards of Article 8 require that in decisions affecting children, the child must be given a meaningful opportunity to be heard and their views must be duly considered in accordance with their age and maturity.

##### b) Application to the Present Case

  1. Throughout the domestic proceedings, the courts refused to hear Lucas (who was 15 years old during the trial and 16 during the appeal) or to allow him to express his views. The Higher Regional Court (OLG) Dresden adopted the written submission of the General Prosecutor's Office (GStA) Dresden without modification, which explicitly declared that the minor’s perspective was "not decisive" (nicht maßgebend) for the enforcement of school attendance (see Annex 9, p. 34 and Annex 11, p. 39-40). The core violation is not merely that the child was not heard, but that the court explicitly ruled that his view was legally meaningless.
  2. Furthermore, the domestic courts did not address the substance of the applicant's factual submissions regarding the school abuses. The Local Court Dresden declared the school's deficiencies "legally irrelevant" to the attendance obligation (see Annex 6, p. 19). The Higher Regional Court dismissed the appeal with a formalistic remark that the applicant failed to take Article 7 GG (state school supervision) into account, using this article to justify state coercion while refusing to address the fundamental contradiction that the state itself had abandoned its supervisory duties by suspending evaluations since 2015 (see Annex 11, p. 40). The Federal Constitutional Court declined to accept the complaint without statement of reasons (see Annex 19, p. 100-101). By treating these documented failures as legally irrelevant, the domestic courts failed to examine the core of the applicant's defense, thereby violating the requirement of a fair hearing.

##### a) Applicable Principles

  1. Article 13 guarantees the availability at the national level of a remedy to enforce the substance of the Convention rights. A remedy must be "effective" in practice as well as in law, meaning it must be accessible, low-threshold, and capable of providing direct redress.

##### b) Application to the Present Case

  1. The State provides no independent, low-threshold complaint mechanism for parents or, particularly, for minor children. The school supervisory authority (LASUB) acts as both the operator and the supervisor of the public schools, creating an inherent conflict of interest. When the applicant petitioned the authority, it actively shielded the school and rejected the complaints (see Annex 1, p. 1-2 and Annex 3, p. 8-9).
  2. The judicial system offers only a circular remedy trap. Under German administrative court practice (Schutznormtheorie), parents and students lack standing to sue for compliance with school quality standards, as these are classified as non-justiciable "programmatic principles" (see Annex 14, p. 77). Even if standing were granted, suing the state carries prohibitive financial risks, and legal aid (Prozesskostenhilfe) is systematically refused because such lawsuits are deemed to lack "prospects of success" under § 114 ZPO, creating a financial barrier to justice. Furthermore, administrative lawsuits take between 1.5 and 3 years, ensuring that cases are routinely dismissed as moot (Erledigung) once the school year ends. Meanwhile, in the repressive enforcement proceedings, the courts declare the school’s failures "legally irrelevant" (see Annex 6, p. 19). This circular referral insulates the executive's illegal practice from any judicial review.
  3. Finally, the state forces parents into an unreasonable requirement of prior compliance (unzumutbare Vorleistungspflicht): they must keep the child in the harmful, abusive environment for the entire duration of the slow administrative litigation, or face immediate fines and coercive detention. The lack of any immediate legal shield or temporary relief mechanism during litigation leaves families with no legal recourse to protect the child's safety, violating the very essence of Article 13.

Conclusion: A Novel Convention Question and Relief Sought

The present application does not challenge the principle of compulsory education. Rather, it challenges the absence of those minimum safeguards that render such state coercion compatible with the Convention.

Heretofore, the Court has examined the compatibility of compulsory school attendance under the assumption that the domestic authorities fulfill their positive obligations of protection and quality assurance. The present case, for the first time, raises the fundamental question of which minimum safeguards must apply when a Contracting State actively abandons these protective mechanisms.

Accordingly, the present application invites the Court to clarify whether compulsory school attendance remains compatible with the Convention where the State simultaneously:

  • abandons objective quality assurance,
  • refuses to hear the minor child,
  • provides no effective remedy against administrative abuses or educational failure, and
  • nevertheless continues to enforce physical attendance through severe coercive measures.

Consequently, the applicant respectfully requests the Court to:

  1. Declare the application admissible;
  2. Find a violation of Article 2 of Protocol No. 1, and Articles 6, 8, 10, and 13 of the Convention;
  3. Clarify the minimum positive obligations incumbent upon Contracting States when enforcing compulsory education through a public education monopoly; and
  4. Award the applicant just satisfaction under Article 41 of the Convention for non-pecuniary damage and costs incurred in the domestic and international proceedings.

The question before the Court is therefore not whether a State may require children to attend school. It is whether a State that compels attendance may do so without ensuring that the institutions it forces children to attend remain subject to effective quality oversight, meaningful remedies, and respect for the rights of the child.

Respectfully submitted,

Martin Heinrich

Dresden, 14 July 2026
